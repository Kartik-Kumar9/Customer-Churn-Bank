Model Features Explained

Understanding the data points used to evaluate customer churn risk.

Credit Score

A numerical expression based on a level analysis of a person's credit files, representing the creditworthiness of an individual. A higher score typically indicates lower churn risk as financially stable customers tend to be more loyal.

Location

The country where the customer resides. Significant demographic or economic differences between countries can influence overall banking satisfaction and available alternatives.

Gender

The gender of the customer. Used as a demographic variable to understand if churn patterns vary between male and female customer segments.

Age

Customer's age in years. Older customers generally have more stable banking relationships, whereas younger customers might be more likely to switch for better offers.

Tenure

The number of years the customer has been with the bank. Long-term customers usually have lower churn rates due to accrued benefits and the inconvenience of switching banks.

Account Balance

The monetary value sitting in the customer's accounts. Customers with higher balances may have complex needs yielding either high loyalty or high flight risk if unsatisfied.

Number of Products

How many different bank products (loans, credit cards, savings, checking) the customer uses. More products indicate a deeper relationship, making churn less likely.

Estimated Salary

The customer's estimated annual income. Helps segment customers and understand if financial health correlates with their likelihood to leave the bank.

Has Credit Card

Whether the customer holds a credit card with the bank. Credit cards increase stickiness and integration with the bank's ecosystem.

Active Member

Indicates if the customer frequently logs in, makes transactions, or interacts with the bank. Active members are generally much less likely to churn.
